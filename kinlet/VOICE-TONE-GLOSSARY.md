# Kinlet Voice, Tone & Caregiver Language Guide

**Product**: Kinlet  
**Audience**: Family caregivers (primarily caring for aging parents with Alzheimer's/dementia)  
**Purpose**: Ensure authentic, resonant language across all communications  
**Last Updated**: February 27, 2026

---

## Overview

This guide ensures Kinlet speaks **like a fellow caregiver, not a healthcare company**. Every word should sound like it came from someone who's lived the experience, not someone selling a solution.

**Core Principle**: We're peer-to-peer, not expert-to-novice.

---

## Voice & Tone Framework

### Kinlet's Voice (Constant Across All Channels)

**Who We Sound Like**: A fellow caregiver who's been through it and found something that helps

**Voice Attributes**:
- **Honest**: We don't sugarcoat how hard caregiving is
- **Warm**: We lead with empathy and understanding
- **Peer-level**: We're in this together, not above you
- **Practical**: We focus on real, tested solutions
- **Calm**: We don't add urgency or pressure to already stressed caregivers
- **Private**: We respect the need for safe spaces

**Voice Is NOT**:
- Clinical or medical (we're not doctors)
- Inspirational or motivational (we're not toxic positivity)
- Corporate or salesy (we're not a business selling to you)
- Urgent or demanding (caregivers have enough pressure)
- Preachy or advice-giving (we share experience, not instructions)

---

### Tone (Adjusts by Context)

**When Someone Is Struggling** (Crisis Posts, Burnout Content):
- **Tone**: Validating, gentle, no solutions-pushing
- **Language**: "This is so hard." "You're not alone." "It's okay to feel this way."
- **Avoid**: "Have you tried...?" "You should..." "It gets better."
- **Example**: "I read your post and just wanted to say—this is really hard. And you're allowed to feel overwhelmed. I've been there too."

**When Someone Is Seeking Advice** (Question Posts):
- **Tone**: Helpful, specific, humble
- **Language**: "Here's what worked for me..." "Every situation is different, but..." "I learned..."
- **Avoid**: "You need to..." "The answer is..." "I know exactly what you should do."
- **Example**: "I dealt with something similar with my mom. What helped us was [specific thing], but I know every parent is different."

**When Sharing Kinlet** (Introduction, CTA):
- **Tone**: Natural, non-salesy, story-driven
- **Language**: "I found something that's helped me..." "I'm building..." "If you're interested..."
- **Avoid**: "You should join..." "This will solve..." "Limited time..."
- **Example**: "I started connecting with a few other caregivers in similar situations, and it's made the hard days more manageable. Happy to share more if you're interested."

**When Celebrating Small Wins** (Positive Posts):
- **Tone**: Warm, realistic, grounded
- **Language**: "Small win today..." "Not a big deal, but..." "Felt good for a minute..."
- **Avoid**: "Everything is great!" "So blessed!" "Best day ever!"
- **Example**: "My dad smiled at a photo today. Didn't remember the person in it, but he smiled. I'll take it."

## Caregiver Language Glossary

### Terms Caregivers Actually Use

**Disease & Condition Terms**:
- ✅ **"Alzheimer's"** (most common, not "Alzheimer's disease")
- ✅ **"dementia"** (lowercase, generic term)
- ✅ **"vascular dementia"**, **"Lewy body dementia"** (when being specific)
- ✅ **"early/mid/late stage"** (staging terminology caregivers use)
- ✅ **"cognitive decline"** (when being gentle/clinical)
- ❌ Avoid: "Senile", "senility", "elderly dementia" (outdated, stigmatizing)

**Relationship Terms**:
- ✅ **"my mom"**, **"my dad"** (personal, not "my mother")
- ✅ **"my parent"** (when being inclusive)
- ✅ **"my loved one"** (formal but acceptable in written content)
- ✅ **"my person"** (sometimes used, very intimate)
- ❌ Avoid: "patient", "elder", "senior", "your loved one" (too clinical or distancing)

**Caregiving Role Terms**:
- ✅ **"caregiver"** (primary term, not "caretaker")
- ✅ **"primary caregiver"** (when distinguishing from family helpers)
- ✅ **"family caregiver"** (to distinguish from professional)
- ✅ **"adult child"** (caregiving relationship, not "son/daughter caring for parent")
- ✅ **"caring for my mom"** (verb form, active)
- ❌ Avoid: "caretaker" (less common in caregiver communities), "guardian" (legal term)

**Behavioral & Symptom Terms**:
- ✅ **"sundowning"** (agitation in late afternoon/evening)
- ✅ **"repetitive questions"** or **"the loop"** (asking same thing repeatedly)
- ✅ **"wandering"** (leaving home unsafely)
- ✅ **"confusion"**, **"disorientation"** (cognitive symptoms)
- ✅ **"agitation"**, **"aggression"** (behavioral symptoms)
- ✅ **"hallucinations"**, **"delusions"** (clinical but widely understood)
- ✅ **"not recognizing me"** or **"didn't know who I was"** (identity confusion)
- ❌ Avoid: "acting out", "being difficult", "combative" (stigmatizing)

**Emotional Experience Terms**:
- ✅ **"grief"** (even though person is still alive)
- ✅ **"ambiguous loss"** (grieving someone still present)
- ✅ **"caregiver guilt"** (universal experience)
- ✅ **"caregiver burnout"** (extreme exhaustion)
- ✅ **"isolation"** (feeling alone despite having people around)
- ✅ **"overwhelm"** (noun and verb form)
- ✅ **"drowning"** (metaphor for being overwhelmed)
- ✅ **"invisible"** or **"unseen"** (feeling unrecognized)

**Practical Care Terms**:
- ✅ **"ADLs"** (activities of daily living - bathing, dressing, eating)
- ✅ **"respite care"** (temporary relief for caregiver)
- ✅ **"memory care"** or **"memory care facility"** (specialized dementia care)
- ✅ **"assisted living"** (residential care with support)
- ✅ **"nursing home"** (most people still use this, not "skilled nursing facility")
- ✅ **"day program"** or **"adult day care"** (daytime activities/supervision)
- ✅ **"home care"** or **"in-home care"** (professional help at home)

**Time & Duration Terms**:
- ✅ **"I've been caring for [X years/months]"** (how long they've been caregiving)
- ✅ **"since diagnosis"** (marking time from diagnosis date)
- ✅ **"good days and bad days"** (fluctuating experience)
- ✅ **"hard days"** (difficult days, not "bad days")
- ✅ **"the long goodbye"** (Alzheimer's progression metaphor)

**Support & Resource Terms**:
- ✅ **"support group"** (generic term for any group support)
- ✅ **"caregiver support"** (broad term for resources)
- ✅ **"peer support"** (caregiver-to-caregiver, Kinlet's positioning)
- ✅ **"online community"** (Reddit, Facebook groups, forums)
- ✅ **"forum"** (traditional online discussion space)
- ❌ Avoid: "platform", "network", "community app" (too tech-focused)

## Phrases Caregivers Actually Say

### Common Expressions (Use These)

**About the Experience**:
- "I'm in the thick of it right now"
- "Some days are harder than others"
- "It's a roller coaster"
- "Nobody tells you about [X]"
- "I wish someone had told me..."
- "No one understands unless they've done it"
- "I feel like I'm the only one going through this"
- "It's the hardest thing I've ever done"
- "I love them, but this is so hard"
- "I'm grieving someone who's still here"

**About Seeking Help**:
- "I need people who get it"
- "I just need to vent"
- "I don't even know what to ask for"
- "Has anyone else dealt with this?"
- "How do you cope with...?"
- "What do I even do with this?"
- "I can't keep doing this alone"
- "I need someone to talk to who understands"

**About Daily Reality**:
- "Today was a 10/10 difficulty day"
- "I answered the same question 50 times today"
- "She didn't recognize me again today"
- "The sundowning is getting worse"
- "I'm running on empty"
- "I haven't slept in [X]"
- "I lost my patience today"
- "I feel like a terrible caregiver"

**About Small Wins**:
- "Small win today"
- "Not a big deal, but..."
- "He smiled today and it made my week"
- "We had a good moment"
- "I'll take what I can get"
- "Little victories"

**About Isolation**:
- "Nobody in my life understands"
- "My friends don't get it"
- "I can't talk about this with anyone"
- "I'm doing this alone"
- "It's lonely even when you're not alone"
- "I feel invisible"

## Language to AVOID

### Clinical/Medical Language

❌ "Cognitive impairment" → ✅ "memory loss" or "confusion"  
❌ "Patient" → ✅ "my mom", "my dad", "my parent"  
❌ "Exhibit behaviors" → ✅ "doing [specific behavior]"  
❌ "Non-compliant" → ✅ "refusing" or "not wanting to"  
❌ "Neurological decline" → ✅ "getting worse" or "progressing"

### Corporate/Marketing Language

❌ "Platform" → ✅ "place" or "space"  
❌ "Solution" → ✅ "something that helps"  
❌ "Transform your caregiving" → ✅ "make hard days more manageable"  
❌ "Join our community" → ✅ "connect with other caregivers"  
❌ "Exclusive access" → ✅ "early access" or just "access"  
❌ "Revolutionary" → ✅ "different" or "new approach"  
❌ "Care recipient" → ✅ "my mom", "my dad", "who you're caring for"  
❌ "Caregiving journey" → ✅ "what you're going through"  
❌ "Perfect match" → ✅ "group"  
❌ "Micro-group" → ✅ "small group"

### Toxic Positivity Language

❌ "You've got this!" → ✅ "This is really hard"  
❌ "Stay positive!" → ✅ "It's okay to struggle"  
❌ "Everything happens for a reason" → ✅ "This isn't fair"  
❌ "At least they're still here" → ✅ "Grief is still grief"  
❌ "You're so strong" → ✅ "You're doing your best"  
❌ "God never gives you more than you can handle" → ✅ "You're allowed to feel overwhelmed"

### Advice-Giving Language (Unless Asked)

❌ "You should..." → ✅ "I found..." or "What worked for me..."  
❌ "Have you tried...?" → ✅ "I tried [X] and it helped"  
❌ "You need to..." → ✅ "I learned that..."  
❌ "The answer is..." → ✅ "Here's what I've seen work..."  
❌ "If I were you..." → ✅ "When I was in that situation..."

## Writing Examples: Before & After

### Example 1: Introducing Kinlet

❌ **Before** (Corporate, Salesy):
"Join Kinlet's revolutionary platform for caregiver support. Our community transforms the caregiving experience through innovative peer connections."

✅ **After** (Authentic, Peer-to-Peer):
"I'm building Kinlet to help caregivers find others in similar situations. Small groups, private conversations. No one should go through this alone."

### Example 2: Responding to Struggle Post

❌ **Before** (Advice-Giving, Toxic Positivity):
"Have you tried joining a support group? You should really find a therapist. Remember, self-care is so important! You're doing great—stay strong!"

✅ **After** (Validating, Peer-Level):
"This is so hard. The isolation piece hits different—people care, but they don't understand. I've been there too. You're allowed to feel overwhelmed."

### Example 3: DM Outreach

❌ **Before** (Sales Pitch):
"Hi! I noticed you're a caregiver. I'd like to tell you about Kinlet, an amazing new platform that will revolutionize your caregiving journey. Can I send you a link?"

✅ **After** (Personal, Curious):
"Hey, I saw your comment about feeling isolated in caregiving. I've been there too—it's the hardest part. I'm connecting with a few other caregivers in similar situations and it's helping. Happy to share more if you're interested."

## Content Creation Checklist

Before publishing ANY content, ask:

**Voice Check**:
- [ ] Does this sound like a fellow caregiver, not a company?
- [ ] Would I actually say this to another caregiver in person?
- [ ] Is the tone appropriate for the context (struggle vs advice vs celebration)?

**Language Check**:
- [ ] Am I using terms caregivers actually use? (Check glossary)
- [ ] Have I avoided clinical/corporate language?
- [ ] Have I avoided toxic positivity or advice-giving?
- [ ] Are there any euphemisms I should replace with direct language?

**Authenticity Check**:
- [ ] Does this come from real experience, not marketing copy?
- [ ] Would this resonate with someone in crisis? Or would it feel tone-deaf?
- [ ] Am I being honest about how hard this is?
- [ ] Am I peer-to-peer, not expert-to-novice?

**Kinlet-Specific Check**:
- [ ] If mentioning Kinlet, is it natural and story-driven (not salesy)?
- [ ] Am I leading with value/empathy before any product mention?
- [ ] Have I avoided "revolutionary", "platform", "solution" language?

---

## Golden Rule

**If you wouldn't say it to a fellow caregiver at a support group meeting, don't write it in content.**

Caregivers have finely-tuned BS detectors. Be the one who actually gets it.
